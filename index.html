A Brief History

French fries — also called frites, frieten, patat, or simply chips — are batonnet-cut deep-fried potatoes of disputed origin. Both Belgium and France lay claim to inventing this golden delight.

The standard cooking method involves a two-stage frying process: an initial bath at around 160°C cooks the potato through, while a second hotter bath at 190°C creates that irresistibly crispy exterior thanks to the Maillard reaction.

From Belgian frietkots to Parisian bistros, from Dutch patatje oorlog to Canadian poutine — fries have become a global culinary icon enjoyed in hundreds of variations across the world.

Did you know?

  • 🇧🇪 Belgium has a museum dedicated entirely to fries — the Frietmuseum in Bruges.
  • 🇺🇸 The average American eats about 14 kg of fries per year.
  • 🇨🇦 Florenceville-Bristol calls itself "the French fry capital of the world".
  • 🥔 The Maillard reaction is what gives fries their golden color.
